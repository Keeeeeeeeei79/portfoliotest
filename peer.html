Reflecting on Peer Feedback: Finding My Voice in Group Settings

As part of my ICL studies, I had the opportunity to receive peer feedback from fellow students on my participation in group work and class discussions. Reflecting on this feedback has been one of the more personally challenging — and ultimately rewarding — aspects of the programme.

The feedback I received generally acknowledged my commitment and work ethic: peers noted that I was reliable and thorough in completing my share of group tasks. However, one recurring theme was that I tended to hold back during verbal discussions, and that my contributions were stronger in written form than in spoken exchanges. One classmate noted that they felt I had more to offer than I was sharing, and that the group would have benefited from hearing my perspective more directly.

Initially, reading this feedback made me feel somewhat defensive — I was aware of this tendency but had attributed it to language barriers. Reflecting more deeply, however, I recognise that while language is a genuine factor, there is also an element of cultural conditioning. In Japanese professional and academic settings, it is generally considered respectful to listen and defer to others before speaking, particularly in group settings. In a New Zealand context, however, active participation and confident contribution are typically valued and expected.

This feedback has directly influenced how I approach participation. In subsequent classes, I have made a deliberate effort to contribute verbally — even if imperfectly — rather than waiting until I feel completely confident. I have also practised preparing one or two specific points before group discussions, so that I have something concrete to share rather than speaking entirely off the top of my head.

The change has not been instant, but I have noticed improvement. More importantly, I have come to value peer feedback as a genuinely useful mirror that reveals blind spots I cannot easily see myself. Going forward, I intend to actively seek this kind of feedback in professional settings as well — particularly as I move into a new industry where I will need to establish credibility in a different way.

Actions Taken & Planned
01
Prepare 2–3 talking points before each class discussion or group session, so I have something concrete to contribute from the start.
02
Speak up earlier in conversations rather than waiting for a "perfect" moment — practise contributing even when uncertain.
03
Reframe verbal contribution as a professional skill to actively practise, not a performance to get right — imperfect input has value.
04
Continue seeking peer feedback in group projects and professional settings — treat it as a development tool, not a judgement.
