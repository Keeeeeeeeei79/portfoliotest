One of the most impactful learnings I gained during the ICL Leading & Management course was the study of key leadership theories — in particular, transformational leadership and situational leadership. Before this course, I had observed leadership in action throughout my years working as a physiotherapist in Japan, but I had never had a formal framework to understand why some leaders were more effective than others, or how leadership style could be deliberately adapted.

In the course, we explored how transformational leaders motivate people by appealing to shared values and inspiring a collective vision, while situational leaders adjust their style depending on the capability and readiness of each team member. This distinction resonated strongly with me. Looking back on my clinical career, I could now identify leaders I had worked under and understand — using these frameworks — why certain management styles were more or less effective in different situations.

What made this learning especially useful was how immediately applicable it was to my current work environment in Auckland. Working at a café, I began to consciously apply situational leadership principles: offering more guidance and structure to new team members, while giving experienced colleagues greater autonomy. I noticed that this approach improved communication and reduced frustration in the team during busy periods.

Going forward, I plan to continue applying these theories as I move into business environments. Understanding leadership at a conceptual level — and being able to reflect on and adjust one's approach — is not just relevant for managers, but for anyone who hopes to contribute meaningfully within an organisation. This has shaped how I think about my future career in marketing and data analytics, where influencing others without formal authority is often essential.

Another significant learning from my ICL studies was the role of communication — and particularly cross-cultural communication — in effective team management. In the Leading & Management course, we examined how different communication styles, whether high-context or low-context, direct or indirect, can lead to misunderstandings in diverse teams.

This was personally meaningful to me as a Japanese professional living and working in New Zealand. Coming from a high-context cultural background, where much is left unsaid and understood implicitly, I found New Zealand's more direct communication style initially challenging. The course gave me a language and a framework to understand these differences not as deficiencies, but as different cultural norms that need to be recognised and bridged.

In practical terms, this learning helped me at work. I became more intentional about expressing my thoughts directly and clearly, rather than hinting or staying quiet to avoid disruption — a habit common in Japanese professional culture. I also became more empathetic toward colleagues from other cultural backgrounds who might experience similar adjustment challenges.

This skill is something I see as critical for my future career. In data analytics and marketing roles in New Zealand, I will regularly collaborate with diverse teams and communicate complex information to stakeholders with varying levels of technical knowledge. The ability to adapt my communication style to different audiences is a competency I am actively building, and this course gave me a strong conceptual foundation to do so.

One learning that has had a lasting impact on me is the practice of structured goal-setting — specifically using frameworks like SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound). I encountered this concept through my ICL studies, and while the idea is relatively straightforward, the discipline of actually applying it to my own professional development was something entirely new.

Prior to my studies, my approach to career planning was largely informal and reactive. I knew I wanted to achieve more and change direction, but my goals were vague: "I want to work in business" or "I want to learn more about data." Going through the process of articulating specific, measurable learning goals — as required in this portfolio — forced me to think more carefully about what I actually want, and what steps will realistically get me there.

This shift in thinking has been immediately useful. By identifying clear targets — such as completing a digital marketing certification or improving my data skills in Excel — I have started prioritising my learning time more effectively. Rather than feeling overwhelmed by everything I don't know, I have a clearer roadmap that makes progress feel achievable.

Looking ahead, strategic thinking and goal-setting are skills I see as directly transferable to the types of roles I am pursuing. A marketing analyst needs to set measurable campaign objectives; a business analyst needs to define clear project outcomes. Practising these habits now, as a student, is laying the groundwork for how I will operate as a professional.

A key insight from my ICL coursework has been learning how to bridge the gap between academic theory and real-world professional practice. Coming from a clinical healthcare background in Japan, I was trained to apply evidence-based protocols — but the connection between management theory and everyday workplace behaviour was less intuitive to me when I began my business studies.

The Leading & Management course challenged me to do more than simply understand theories: it encouraged me to observe, analyse, and reflect on actual organisational behaviour through those theoretical lenses. For example, when reading about organisational culture and its influence on employee motivation, I found myself thinking critically about the café where I work — how the manager's communication style shapes the team's morale, how informal norms influence who speaks up and who stays quiet.

This reflective habit has been genuinely valuable. It has made my coursework feel relevant and grounded rather than abstract, and it has made my day-to-day work feel more analytically rich. I am now more observant in professional settings — asking myself why certain dynamics exist, and what might improve them.

As I move toward a career in marketing or data analysis, this ability to connect frameworks to real contexts will be essential. Data does not exist in a vacuum; interpreting it meaningfully requires understanding the human and organisational context. The habit of theory-to-practice thinking, which I have developed through ICL, is one I will carry throughout my career.

Perhaps the most unexpected — but ultimately most significant — learning of my time in New Zealand has been the development of personal resilience and adaptability. Moving to a new country, studying in a second language, working in hospitality while managing the demands of academic coursework, and navigating an entirely unfamiliar professional culture: all of these have required me to build a level of adaptability I did not anticipate needing.

In the context of my ICL studies, I have come to understand resilience not just as a personal trait, but as a professional competency. Management literature increasingly recognises that individuals who can manage uncertainty, recover from setbacks, and continue to perform under pressure are highly valued in modern organisations. Living this experience has given me a deep, experiential understanding of what resilience means in practice.

This learning has been useful in multiple ways. Academically, it has taught me to persist through difficult assignments and to ask for help when needed — rather than suffering in silence, as I might have done in Japan. Professionally, it has given me genuine examples I can draw on in job applications and interviews to demonstrate my ability to adapt and perform across different cultural and professional contexts.

One specific application has been in my approach to feedback. Early in my studies, receiving critical feedback on my work felt uncomfortable. Over time, I have reframed feedback as a tool for improvement, and I now actively seek it. This shift in mindset — from viewing challenge as a threat to viewing it as an opportunity — is one I see as central to long-term professional success, particularly as I navigate a career change into a new industry.
